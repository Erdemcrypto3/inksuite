# Layer 2s Won. Here's Why Nobody Uses Ethereum Directly Anymore.

Remember paying $80 in gas to swap tokens on Uniswap? Yeah, that era is over.

## The Great Migration

In 2023, most DeFi activity happened on Ethereum mainnet. By 2026, the picture looks completely different. Base, Arbitrum, Optimism, Ink — Layer 2s now handle the majority of everyday transactions.

The reason isn't complicated: why would you pay $5-20 for a transaction when you can pay $0.01 for the same thing with the same security?

## What Actually Changed

Three things made L2s take over:

**1. Costs dropped to basically zero.** EIP-4844 (the "blob" upgrade) slashed L2 data costs by 90%+. A swap on Ink or Base costs less than a penny. That opened up use cases that were impossible before — micro-tips, gaming transactions, daily DeFi activity.

**2. Bridges got better.** The early bridge experience was terrifying. Send tokens into the void, wait 20 minutes, pray they arrive. Now it's mostly one-click with near-instant finality. Superchain networks like Ink can even share liquidity natively.

**3. Apps moved first.** When Uniswap, Aave, and other protocols deployed on L2s, users followed. Nobody wants to use the expensive version when a cheaper one exists with the same interface.

## The Superchain Effect

Here's the part most people miss: L2s aren't competing with each other as much as it seems. The ones built on Optimism's OP Stack (Base, Ink, Optimism, Mode, Zora) are forming something called the Superchain.

Think of it like this: each chain is a city, and the Superchain is the highway system connecting them. Assets and messages flow between chains without traditional bridges. If you're on Base and want to interact with something on Ink, it'll eventually be as seamless as switching tabs.

Kraken building Ink on the OP Stack wasn't random. They're betting that the Superchain network effect will be the winning play.

## What This Means for You

If you're a user: you should be on L2s. There's no good reason to transact on Ethereum mainnet for most activities. Pick one or two L2s, bridge some ETH over, and enjoy sub-penny fees.

If you're a builder: deploy on L2s. The user base is there, the costs are lower, and the grant programs (like Ink's Spark) are actively funding new projects.

If you're an investor: watch TVL flows. Money is moving from mainnet to L2s, and within L2s, from older ones to newer ones with better incentives. Ink went from $7M to $450M TVL in four months. That kind of growth creates opportunities.

## The Catch

L2s aren't perfect. Sequencers are still mostly centralized (one entity ordering transactions). Cross-chain UX still has friction. And the sheer number of L2s is fragmenting liquidity.

But these are solvable problems, and teams are actively working on them. Shared sequencers, chain abstraction, unified liquidity layers — all in development.

The direction is clear. The details are what's left to figure out.
