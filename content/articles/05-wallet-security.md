# Your Wallet Security Checklist (Because Nobody Will Bail You Out)

Here's the uncomfortable truth about crypto: if someone steals your tokens, they're gone. No customer support line. No charge-back. No "fraud department" to call.

But most hacks aren't sophisticated. They're preventable mistakes. Here's how to not be the next cautionary tale.

## The Basics (Do These Today)

### Never share your seed phrase. Ever.

This sounds obvious until you realize how creative scammers are. They'll pretend to be support agents, create fake wallet apps that ask for your seed, or build sites that look exactly like MetaMask asking you to "verify" your recovery phrase.

**The rule is absolute:** No legitimate service, protocol, or support team will ever ask for your seed phrase. If someone asks, they're trying to rob you. Period.

### Use a hardware wallet for anything significant

If you have more than a few hundred dollars in crypto, get a hardware wallet (Ledger, Trezor). Your hot wallet (MetaMask, Rabby) is for daily use with small amounts. Your hardware wallet is the vault.

Think of it like cash vs. bank: you carry some cash in your pocket for coffee, but you don't walk around with your life savings in a backpack.

### Separate your wallets by purpose

- **Hot wallet #1:** Daily DeFi, minting, testing new protocols. Small amounts only.
- **Hot wallet #2:** Holding mid-term positions. Doesn't interact with sketchy contracts.
- **Hardware wallet:** Long-term holdings. Rarely connects to anything.

If wallet #1 gets compromised, your other assets are safe.

## Approvals: The Silent Killer

When you use a DeFi protocol, you often "approve" it to spend your tokens. Most people click approve without thinking. The problem? Many approvals are unlimited — meaning the contract can move ALL of your tokens of that type, forever.

**What to do:**
- Set custom approval amounts instead of unlimited when possible
- Regularly check your approvals using tools like Revoke.cash
- Revoke approvals for protocols you no longer use
- Before approving anything, ask: do I trust this contract with all my tokens?

## Common Attack Vectors

### Phishing sites
Fake versions of real protocols. The URL looks almost right — uniswap.com instead of app.uniswap.org, for example. Always bookmark the real sites and use those bookmarks.

### Discord/Telegram scams
"New mint going live!" links in DMs. Official projects will never DM you first. Turn off DMs from server members.

### Malicious airdrops
Random tokens appear in your wallet. You try to sell them on a DEX, and the approval drains your real tokens. **Never interact with tokens you didn't buy or earn from a known source.**

### Clipboard hijacking
Malware that replaces crypto addresses in your clipboard. You copy one address, paste another. Always double-check the first and last few characters of any address before sending.

## The 5-Second Rule

Before you sign any transaction, take 5 seconds to read what it's actually doing. Your wallet shows you the function name, the contract address, and what tokens are being moved.

If you don't understand what you're signing, don't sign it. Close the tab, research the protocol, ask in their community channels, and come back when you understand.

Five seconds of reading can save you thousands of dollars.

## Recovery Plan

Assume the worst will happen and plan for it:

- Store your seed phrase offline, in multiple locations, on metal (not paper)
- Have a plan for what to do if your hot wallet is compromised (which assets to move first)
- Keep a list of your active approvals
- Set up alerts for large transfers from your wallets
