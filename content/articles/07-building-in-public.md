# Why I'm Building 10 Apps in One Week (And Publishing Everything)

Last week I decided to build a suite of small apps on Ink — Kraken's new Layer 2. Not one app. Not two. Ten.

Sounds crazy? Maybe. But here's my thinking.

## The Problem With "One Big Thing"

Most builders in crypto try to create the next Uniswap or the next OpenSea. They spend months on a single product, raise money, hire a team, and then launch to... crickets. The market moved on. The thing they built isn't what people actually need.

I think there's a better approach for solo builders: ship many small things, see what sticks.

## The Suite Approach

Instead of building one complex app, I'm building Ink Suite — a collection of focused tools under one domain. Each app does one thing well:

- A gas tracker that shows real-time Ink gas prices
- A wallet dashboard for checking your on-chain activity
- A quiz game with achievement NFTs
- A fantasy football squad builder
- A daily on-chain gm widget
- A decentralized blog platform
- An AI NFT generator

And more. Each one takes a day or two to build because I'm reusing a shared template, shared wallet connection, and shared design system across all of them.

## Why Open Source Everything

The entire codebase is public on GitHub. Every commit, every design decision, every bug fix — visible to anyone. Why?

First, trust. Crypto has a reputation problem. Open source lets anyone verify that the code does what it claims to do.

Second, learning. When I was starting out, I learned more from reading other people's code than from any tutorial. If my code helps someone else ship their first DeFi app, that's a win.

Third, accountability. When your code is public, you write better code. You think twice before cutting corners because you know someone might actually read it.

## What I've Learned So Far

**Shipping beats planning.** My original plan had 15 apps carefully designed in Figma. By day two, I threw out the plan and just started building. The apps that exist now are better than what I planned because real usage feedback shaped them.

**Layer 2s are a builder's paradise.** Gas costs nothing on Ink. Deploy a contract for cents, interact with it for fractions of a cent. The feedback loop between "write code" and "see it work on-chain" is almost instant.

**Small tools get more love than you'd think.** The gas tracker is dead simple — it just shows the current gas price. But people actually use it. Not every app needs to be revolutionary. Sometimes useful is enough.

**One shared codebase is a superpower.** Every new app inherits the wallet connection, the design system, the deployment pipeline. App #8 took a fraction of the time of app #1.

## The Honest Part

Not all of these apps will survive. Some will get zero users. Some might have bugs I haven't found yet. Building fast means accepting imperfection.

But the alternative — spending six months polishing one thing that might not work anyway — feels worse. At least this way, I'm learning, shipping, and finding out what people actually want.

If any of these apps gets real traction, I'll double down on that one. The suite is a discovery mechanism, not an end state.
