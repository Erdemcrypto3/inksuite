# NFTs Are Dead. Long Live NFTs.

The $69 million Beeple sale. Bored Apes everywhere. "Right-click save" arguments on Twitter. That era of NFTs is over, and honestly, good riddance.

But NFTs as technology? They're just getting started.

## What Went Wrong (And Right)

The 2021-2022 NFT bubble was mostly about speculation. People bought JPEGs they didn't care about, hoping to flip them for profit. When the music stopped, 95% of collections went to zero. Surprise.

What survived are the projects that had actual utility or genuine cultural significance. And quietly, builders have been using NFT technology for things that actually make sense.

## Where NFTs Actually Work

### Digital Membership & Access
Instead of a plastic gym card or an email subscription, imagine a token in your wallet that proves your membership. It's transferable, verifiable on-chain, and can't be counterfeited. Some communities are already doing this — token-gated Discord channels were just the beginning.

### Gaming Items
Your sword in a game shouldn't disappear when the company shuts down their servers. NFTs make game items truly yours. You can trade them, sell them, or use them across different games that support the same standard.

This isn't hypothetical anymore. Games on chains like Ink are experimenting with inventory systems where items are ERC-721 or ERC-1155 tokens.

### Proof of Attendance & Achievement
Were you at that conference? Did you complete that course? On-chain credentials are more verifiable than a PDF certificate. POAPs (Proof of Attendance Protocol) have been doing this since 2021, and the concept is expanding.

### Creative Monetization
This is where NFTs still shine for artists and writers. Instead of relying on ads or subscriptions, creators can sell their work directly as collectibles. Platforms like Mirror (now Paragraph) let writers mint articles. Musicians release songs as limited editions.

The key difference from the bubble era: people buy because they genuinely value the work, not because they expect to flip it for 10x.

### On-Chain Identity
Your wallet address is boring. But attach a collection of NFTs that represent your achievements, memberships, contributions, and credentials — now it tells a story. This is the foundation of on-chain reputation systems.

## The Technology Keeps Improving

ERC-721 was the original NFT standard. Then came ERC-1155 for semi-fungible tokens (great for editions and game items). Now there's ERC-6551 (token-bound accounts — NFTs that own other NFTs) and various soulbound token standards for non-transferable credentials.

Minting costs have dropped from $50-100 on Ethereum mainnet to fractions of a cent on L2s. That changes everything. You can now mint a blog post, a game achievement, or a membership card for practically nothing.

## My Prediction

In two years, you'll interact with NFTs daily without calling them NFTs. Your event ticket will be one. Your loyalty points will be one. Your game inventory will be full of them.

The word "NFT" might even disappear, replaced by more specific terms. And that'll be the real sign that the technology has matured — when it's so embedded in how things work that nobody needs to explain it anymore.
